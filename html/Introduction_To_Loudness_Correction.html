What does loudness mean ?

Loudness means the sound level percieved by a human.

Electrical peaks shown by a peak meter do not correspond to how a human percieves the loudness of a sound. Human hearing tends to average sound level a bit. Frequency components and their durations also affect human sensation of loudness.

In this documents when we talk about loudness we always mean the sound level perceived by a person or a measurement done with a loudness meter. When we talk about peak value we mean the electrical measurement of the highest electrical peak of a sound signal.


Problematic loudness

Loudness jumps have been a problem since tv- and radio begun. It is the cause of constant complaint from the viewers.

Despite our technical advancements there hasn't been any proper tools to control loudness. This is why the problem gas remained unsold until our days.

There are two separate problems that needs to be solved:
  1. Loudness jumps inside a program.
  2. Loudness jumps between programs.


Why haven't we been able to solve the loudness problem ?

It has been difficult to model the way humans perceive loudness and this is tha reason we have not had any proper tools to control loudness.

Peak meter has been the only tool to manage loudness. However readings on a peak meter do not match the way how humans hear loudness.

The situation has slowly grown worse. In the analog era we used PPM - meters that had some deliberate 'slowness' on how fast the meter reacted to rapid changes in sound level. This slowness or integration time had an averaging effect on the meter display. This averaging removed sharp movement from the meter display causing the meter to react more like human hearing reacts on the loudness of sound. When the digital age began fast digital meters surpassed PPM meters and we lost the averaging effect of the meter. Readings on a fast digital meter do not correspond any more to how humans 'feel' loudness.

In 1990's digital processing gave sound engineers tools to manipulate audio in ways that were not possible before. The music industry gave birth to a phenomenon were sound was 'over compressed' to gain more and more loudness. Hypercompression was used to get one's own music to sound louder on car radio than the music from cometing companies. This trend soon crossed over to radio and television.

Compression can be used to increase loudness in a way were the electrical peaks in the audio don't go up, in fact they can even go down inspite of loudness going up. This hypercompression made it even more difficult to get a sensible reading for loudness from a peak meter.

Because there has not been any proper tools to manage loudness, the only way for a sound engineer to do it was to use his ears as a loudness meter. Using one's hearing as a loudness meter works surprisingly well, but it's problem is the lack of a commonly agreed target loudness level. A sound engineer can produce very consistent loudness inside the same working day, but the lack of a common standard target level makes it very difficult even for the same sound engineer to produce consistent loudness in the long run.

EBU loudness recommendation R128

The European Broadcasting Union EBU published the loudness recommendation R128 in august 2010. R128 defines a technical method for measuring loudness in a way that corresponds very well with how humans perceive loudness. The recommendation defines a common target loudness level for all audio material and finally gives us the tools to measure and manage loudness.


Main concepts of R128

    • R128 uses a measuring method defined in ITU-R BS 1770. Measurements done with this algorithm correspond very well to human perception of loudness.
    • R128 supports channel counts from one to six (mono -> 5.1).
    • R128 works very well with all types of material (classical music, speech, heavy-metal, etc).
    • R128 uses the same scale used in digital peak meters. In peak meters the unit is dB in loudness meters the unit is LU (Loudness Unit).

Target level

    • Target level for all kinds of  material is -23 LUFS.
    • Kohdeäänekkyystaso on kaikelle äänimateriaalille -23 LUFS.

Loudness scale

    • There are two ways to define the loudness scale (pictire on the left): 1. Relative to target level 2. Relative to maximum level .
    • LU: the maximum value is +23. Unit is LU (Loudness Unit) and target level is 0 LU.
    • LUFS: the maximum value is 0. Unit is LUFS (Loudness Unit Relative to Full Scale) and target level is -23 LUFS.
    • 0 LU = -23 LUFS: Target level is the same in both scales, only the way to apply numbers on the scale are different. The user is free to choose which way he wants to use.

LU vs. dB

    • The R128 uses the same scale as digital peak meters and because of this 1 LU = 1 dB.  This means that if the measured integrated loudness of a program is +3 LU too high, then the loudness will be precisely at target level if you reduce the volume of the mix by 3 dB's.

Countries using R128

  • The following countries (by march 2013) already use or have announced that they will start to use R128 soon (source: http://www.r128audio.com/): France, Germany, Netherlands, Italy, Israel, Switzerland, Austria, Scandinavian countries, Poland. Other European countries will follow shortly.
  • Usa (http://www.thecalmact.com/ ), Japan and Brazil are using a method very similar to R128.


EBU R128 measurement units

The EBU loudness recommendation defines three new measurement units. The main difference in these units is how long the time window of the measurement is.

Integrated loudness

Integrated loudness measures the loudness for the duration of a whole program from start to finish. The result of this measurement is a single number that tells the averaged loudness of the program.
  • The program duration is divided into 400 ms 'slices' and loudness of each slice is measured. Some of the measurement results are thrown away (filtered) according to rules.
  • The back channels of a surround mix get a slightly greater weight in the calculations compared to front channels. The LFE channel is always discarded in the loudness calculations.
  • The result of integrated loudness measurement is a single number that tells the averaged loudness of the program.
  • The target loudness level is -23 LUFS. The integrated loudness of all programs must be at the target level.
  • Loudness is allowed to vary inside a program, only the averaged result of all loudness variations is taken into account.

Short-term loudness

Short-term loudness measures loudness from a 3 second time slice.

  • In case of file based loudness measurement the results of these 3 second time slices are used to create a graphical presentation of loudness variations inside a file.

Momentary loudness

  • Momentary loudness measures loudness from a single 400 ms time slice. The results of multiple momentary loudness measurements is used when integrated loudness and short-term loudness is calculated.

Loudness range (LRA)

  • Loudness range (LRA) tells about the wideness of loudness variations in the audio material.
  • Loudness range tries to tell how far apart the loud and quiet parts of a program are from each other. LRA measurement is somewhat coarse in nature and can be used only as a guide, it is not absolute in any way. LRA results should be taken with a grain of salt.


How are the different measurement results used ?

File based loudness measurement

  • Integrated loudness: The result of this measurement shows the averaged loudness of the program.
  • Short-term loudness: The result of this measurement are used to create a graphical presentation of loudness variations inside a file
  • Momentary loudness: The result of this measurement is not used for anything visible to a user.  These results are used when integrated loudness and short-term loudness is calculated.
  • Loudness range: The result of this measurement shows about the wideness of loudness variations in the audio material.
Picture below: Short-term loudness results are used to create a graphical presentation of loudness variations inside a file. The brown line in the picture is drawn using results from 3 second time slice loudness measurements.


Realtime loudness measurement

  • Integrated loudness: When using a realtime loudness meter then the user must manually start the integrated loudness measurement in the beginning of the program and stop at the end of the program. The measurement result shows the accumulated integrated loudness from the beginning of the program to the current moment.
  • Short-term loudness: This value shows the loudness of the last 3 seconds. This value can be used to determine the direction where the loudness is heading at the moment.
  • Momentary loudness: This value shows fast movements of the loudness and it is used to draw a metering bar on the display of the loudness meter.
  • Loudness range: The result of this measurement shows about the wideness of loudness variations in the audio material.

There is an example of a realtime loudness meter below (Nugen Audio). The measurement units discussed above can all be found on right side of the meter display. They are from top to bottom:
  • Short-term = loudness of the last 3 seconds.
  • Integrated = the integrated loudess from the beginning of the measurement to current moment.
  • Loudness range
  • The meter bar on the right side of the values shows measurements of momentary loudness.

On the left side of the meter display is a loudness history window. It rolls to the left and shows the history of past momentary loudness measurements. The time window of the history display is user definable and in the case below it shows the history of the last two minutes.

Understanding the units of EBU R128 helps to read the display of a realtime loudness meter.




Demovideo 4: shows a simple realtime loudness meter (ZPlane).

The loudness of the audio in the demo increases from start to the middle of the video and then starts to decrease towards the end. The result of integrated loudness can be seen on the right side of the meter display and the led bar shows results from momentary loudness measuremets. Note how the result from integrated loudness moves very slowly compared to the fast led bar.

The slow response to loudness variations of the integrated measuremets is caused by the fact that integrated loudness is the averaged sum of previous loudness measurements. Short changes in the loudness have no effect on integrated loudness, a large number of changes toward the same direction are needed before the averaged result starts to move into that direction. This means that it is possible that there are large loudness variations inside a file and the integrated loudnes still hits the target level of -23 LUFS. The user still has the responsibility to adjust loudness variations in a program to fit the distribution channel.

Realtime or file based loudness measurement ?

Loudness measurement can be done in two different ways: realtime and file based measurement. Both methods have different target applications.

Realtime loudness measurement

Realtime loudness measurement can be used in places where the user has the possibility to read the loudness meter in realtime and also adjust volume of the sound sources in realtime. This method of loudness metering is best in production environments where the program is recorded live like tv - studios, OB vans, live radio, broadcasting centers, etc.

A realtime meter is not absolutely necessary, but can be useful to a sound engineer in a post production studio. Mixing is traditionally done using ones ears as the loudness meter and this still works very well.

File based loudness measurement

If a program is stored as a file before transmission, then the loudness measurement can be done on the audio file of the program. The file can also be automatically corrected to the target loudness level after doing the loudness measurement.

Both measurement methods

The best loudness meter is still the human ear.  A machine has no sense of what sound is important (for example speech) and what is not (for example the sound of heavy rain). The user is still responsible for adjusting loudness variations inside a program to fit the transmission channel. A loudness meter provides a common 'anchor' for the loudness level of all program material.

Viewer adjusts the volume of their tv -sets based on the loudness of speech. If speech level changes then the viewer must adjust his listening volume again. It is important that speech loudness is at the EBU target level or at least very close to it.

Automatic file based loudness correction

In the capters below I will describe the usage of the free file based automatic loudness correction and measurement tool FreeLCS (http://freelcs.sourceforge.net). FreeLCS is a server on the network where users can drop in files to be automatically loudness corrected. The server also creates a loudness history graphics file which gives visual feedback of the internal loudness variations in a file.

It is very easy to use the server:
  1. Mount the server disk to the users local workstation.
  2. Drag files over to the server.
  3. A couple of minutes later the user can drag loudness corrected versions of the files back to his workstation.
Mounting the server disk to the local workstation is discussed in its own chapter. The example below is for windows 7, but the same principles apply to other operating systems also (OS X, windows xp, etc).

In the picture below files from the local workstation are being copied over to the loudness correction server. The network name of the server is "LoudnessCorrection". Note: only files copied to the root folder of the server will be processed.





Loudness corrected files will appear in folder "00-Corrected_Files", double click to open it.





It takes a couple of minutes for the server to process the files.

The first file to appear is the loudness history graphics file. The end of the file name is always "Loudness_Results_Graphics". The file is in jpeg format and it can be opened for viewing by double clicking on it.

The next file to appear is the loudness corrected audio file. The end of the file name is always  "-23_LUFS".

Now the user can copy the loudness corrected file back to the local workstation and attach it as the final mix of the program.

All files on the server will be automatically deleted after a waiting period that is set during FreeLCS installation. It is by default 8 hours.





Description of folders on the LoudnessCorrection - server

There are three folders on the LoudnessCorrection - server:
  • "Root - folder" or "HotFolder" is on top of the server folder structure and the other two folders described below are both inside this folder. Only files dropped into the root - folder will be processed, the server will not process files dropped to any other folder.
  • "00-Corrected_Files" is the folder where loudness corrected audio files and loudness history graphics files will appear.
  • "00-Calculation_Queue_Information" is used for storing a html progress report ("00-Calculation_Queue_Information.html") that shows the current state of the processing queue. This file is a web page and can be double clicked to open it for viewing in a web browser. The html progress report shows how many files are waiting in the queue for processing and which files are currently being processed upon.
All files on the server will be automatically deleted after a waiting period that is set during FreeLCS installation. It is by default 8 hours.

Html progress report

If file processing seems to take longer than usual, then user can view the "html progress report" to see what causes the delay. The file is located in "00-Calculation_Queue_Information / 00-Calculation_Queue_Information.html" and double clicking on it opens it up in the web browser.

The progress report shows the current state of the servers calculation queue and it updates every five seconds. The top of the page shows how many files are waiting in the queue for processing (14 in the example) and below that names of the next ten files to be processed.

Files currently processed upon are shown in the gray box in the middle of the page. The filenames of the last 100 completed files along with completion times is shown on the bottom of the page.





Sample peak vs. TruePeak

Digitizing an analog signal poses a problem that can cause distortion when the sampled audio is converted back to analog.

In the picture below the blue line represents voltage variations in analog audio and red dots represent times when digital samples are taken from the signal. In the example digital samples are taken exactly when the analog voltage is at its highest peak. The sample rate coincides with the peaks in the signal and the peak values are recorded correctly into the digital data.




Sometimes there can be very fast peaks in the audio that happen before or after samples are taken. Instead of the true values of the peaks in the signal lower values are recorded in the digital data.

The problem may go undetected until audio is converted back to analog or the audio is compressed in a lossy format (mpeg2, mpeg3, aac) for transmission. Then these 'phantom peaks' can appear back in the signal.

A conventional digital peak meter won't show these phantom peaks because the information is not available in digital samples of the audio. When samples are converted back to analog audio then these phantom peaks can go over the maximum allowed level in the DA - converter and introduce distortion to the sound.




ITU recommends a method called "TruePeak" to find these phantom peaks after the signal is already in digital format.  This method calculates new samples between samples taken when digitizing audio and this makes the phantom peaks visible again.

A meter that shows peak using this method uses ascale called dBTP when a conventional sample peak meter uses dBFS.

In practice I have seen phantom peaks 3 dBs higher than the highest sample peak value in the same audio. It is possible for a TruePeak measurement to show values that go over the maximum allowed peak of the dBFS scale (0 dBFS) showing positive TruePeak values for example +3 dBTP. In this case this would mean that the true peak value is 3 dBs over the maximum value of for example a DA - converter and converting a signal like this back to analog would cause distortion in the DA - converter.

EBU R128 recommends always using TruePeak when measuring audio peaks. The maximum allowed value for peaks in R128 is   -1 dBTP (dBs using the TruePeak scale).

Tärkeitä huomioita ÄänekkyysKorjaus - palvelimen käytöstä

Tiedostopohjaisessa äänekkyysmittauksessa on muutamia asioita, jotka on tärkeätä ottaa huomioon.

Äänikanavat

Yhden miksauksen kaikkien äänikanavien pitää olla samassa tiedostossa. Esim. stereomiksauksen äänikanavia ei saa käsitellä niin, että äänekkyyskorjaukseen laitetaan erilliset vasemman ja oikean äänikanavan tiedostot. Tällä tavalla käsiteltyjen käsiteltyjen tiedostojen äänekkyys menee väärälle äänekkyystasolle (3 LU :ta pieleen).

Yhdessä äänitiedostossa saa olla vain yhden miksauksen äänikanavat. Jos ohjelmassa on erillinen stereo- ja 5.1 - miksaus, kumpikin miksaus pitää käsitellä erikseen.

Esim: Kun ohjelmassa on kolme eri äänimiksausta (Stereo Suomi, Stereo Ruotsi, 5.1 Suomi) syntyy tästä seuraavat tiedostot:

Tiedosto 1 = Stereo Suomi (kaksi äänikanavaa)
Tiedosto 2 = Stereo Ruotsi (kaksi äänikanavaa)
Tiedosto 3 = 5.1 Suomi (kuusi äänikanavaa)

Raakamateriaalin käsittely

EBU:n R128 on tarkoitettu vain lähetysvalmiiden ohjelmien äänekkyyden mittaamiseen. Raakamateriaalia ei voi äänekkyyskorjata, sillä jos materiaalista leikataan äänekkyyskorjauksen jälkeen pois osia, muuttuu tiedoston keskimääräinen äänekkyys. Tämä johtuu siitä, että keskimääräinen äänekkyys kertyy tiedostosta tehtyjen lyhyiden mittausten keskiarvosta ja tämä keskiarvo muuttuu kun mittaustuloksia (tiedoston osia) poistetaan. Pääsääntöisesti ÄänekkyysKorjaus - palvelimelle siis laitetaan käsiteltäväksi ainoastaan lähetysvalmiita ohjelmia, joita ei enää muuteta mitenkään.

Poikkeuksen edelliseen tekee makasiinohjelmat. Jos materiaali koostuu esim. samassa paikassa tehdyistä studiojuonnoista ja erillisistä inserteistä, voi juonnot ja insertit korjata niin, että jokainen ohjelmasegmentti korjataan erikseen. Tällä tavalla pääsee eroon segmenttien välisistä mahdollisista äänekkyyseroista. Tässä pitää kuitenkin pitää huolta siitä, että materiaalissa ei ole osia, jotka leikataan myöhemmin pois. Hiljaisuutta ei oteta huomioon äänekkyysmittauksessa, joten mykkiä kohtia voi leikata pois vaikuttamatta tiedoston keskimääräiseen äänekkyyteen.

Ohjelman sisäiset äänekkyysvaihtelut

ÄänekkyysKorjaus-palvelin on työkalu, joka säätää ohjelman äänekkyyden lähetysvirtaan sopivaksi. Äänekkyyskorjaus ei muuta tiedoston sisäisiä äänekkysvaihteluita mitenkään. Jos äänekkyyvaihtelut ovat alun perinkin liian suuria, ne ovet sitä myös äänekkyyskorjauksen jälkeen. Ohjelman työstäjän vastuulla on säätää äänimateriaalin sisäiset äänekkyyserot sopiviksi.

Lähetysvirran keskimääräinen äänekkyystaso laskee

Kanavillamme lähetettävien ohjelmien äänekkyystasoja on tilastoitu parin vuoden ajan. Keskimääräinen äänekkyys vaihtelee ohjelmasta toiseen, mutta kaikkien tv - kanaviemme pitkäaikainen keskimääräinen äänekkyys on asettunut tasoon -21 LUFS. Kun siirrymme käyttämään EBU:n äänekkyyssuositusta, laskee lähetysvirran keskimääräinen äänekkyys n. 2 LU:ta.

R128:n käyttöönotolla on hyviä seuraksia

Kun äänen voimakkuus ankkuroidaan oikealle paikalleen äänekkyyden mukaan, sähköisille huipuille ja äänekkyysvaihteluille eli dynamiikan käytölle jää enemmän tilaa. Kompression liiallinen käyttö vähenee, kun sillä ei saavuteta enää äänekkyyshyötyä ja miksauksista tulee hengittävämpiä ja elävämpiä.

EBU:n äänekkyyssuositus siirtää vastuun kotikuuntelun äänekkyydestä takaisin sinne missä sen olisi pitänyt aina ollakin: katsojalle itselleen. Kun kaikkien ohjelmien keskimääräinen äänekkyys on sama, voi katsoja lopultakin itse päättää kuinka kovaa hän mitäkin ohjelmaa kuuntelee.

Miten EBU R128 - suosituksen käyttöönotto vaikuttaa äänisuunittelijan työhön ?

EBU:n äänekkyyssuosituksen suurin käytännön muutos on se, että äänen kompressoinnilla ei enää saavuta äänekkyyshyötyä. Liiallinen kompressio aiheuttaa ainoastaan äänen laadun heikkenemistä ja kuunteluväsymystä kuuntelijassa.

Katselijoiden kotikuuntelussa käyttämä kuunteluvoimakkuus rajoittaa käytettävissä olevan dynamiikka-alueen koon. Kotikuuntelun äänen voimakkuus on keskimäärin suhteellisen matala, eikä tähän tule minkäänlaista muutosta EBU:n äänekkyyssuosituksen käyttöönoton myötä. Käytettävissä olevan dynamiikka-alueen koko pysyy siis samana kuin ennen EBU:n äänekkyyssuosituksen käyttöönottoa.

EBU:n suositus määrittelee sähköiselle huippuarvolle suurimmaksi sallituksi arvoksi -1 dBTP. Kaikki arvot tämän alapuolella ovat sallittuja. Sähköisen huippuarvon suurimmalla arvolla ei EBU:n suosituksen käyttöönoton jälkeen ole enää kovinkaan suurta merkitystä. Huippuarvo saa olla mikä tahansa kunhan suurin sallittu arvo -1 dBTP ei ylity.

Vanhoja hyväksi todettuja miksausmenetelmiä ei tarvitse muuttaa mitenkään, ne toimivat EBU:n äänekkyyssuosituksen käyttöönoton jälkeen yhtä hyvin kuin ennenkin. Ainoa muutos on se että ohjelmien keskimääräinen äänekkyys pakotetaan samalle tasolle.

Korva on myös edelleen äänittäjän tärkein työkalu ja paras äänekkyysmittari. Kone ei voi tietää mikä on hyötyääntä esim. puhetta ja mikä taustaa.
